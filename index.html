Eco-Climate Laboratory

This single-file application turns the reduced-form notebook into an interactive browser model. It couples a two-box climate response to atmospheric CO₂, a simple ecological land sink, ocean uptake, an optional N₂O module, and a stylized built-surface forcing term representing urban and road expansion. The goal is not precision forecasting, but structural experimentation across centuries.

Scenario
Baseline System
Emissions peak early and decline gradually while urban expansion continues at moderate rates. This run illustrates the intrinsic thermal inertia of the climate system and the slow relaxation of the carbon cycle over centuries.
Infrastructure experiment
Urban Acceleration
Infrastructure expands rapidly and impervious surfaces grow faster than ecological recovery. The simulation highlights how urban form can indirectly amplify warming by weakening land carbon uptake.
Ecological restoration
Restoration Pathway
Large-scale depaving and land restoration increase biological productivity and strengthen terrestrial carbon sinks. This experiment explores whether infrastructure redesign can shift long-term climate trajectories.
Atmospheric chemistry
High N₂O World
Agricultural emissions of nitrous oxide remain high while atmospheric lifetimes lengthen. This scenario explores how trace-gas forcing interacts with CO₂-driven warming over multi-century horizons.

Interpretive notes

The urban and road modules are stylized trajectories rather than empirical reconstructions. Their purpose is to expose one specific structural idea from your notebook: infrastructure can alter climate not only through direct forcing but by shrinking biologically productive land and weakening terrestrial uptake.

Because all components are transparent, you can readily replace any pathway with empirical data, add scenario import hooks, or split the system into multiple tabs such as atmosphere, biosphere, and urban form.
